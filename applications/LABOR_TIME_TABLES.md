### The Core Insight

Labor-time analysis reveals what money obscures: **the true cost of goods in human life.**

- A Nigerian worker must sacrifice 1,200 days of labor for an iPhone; an American, 4 days.
- US healthcare costs have exploded 7x in labor-hours since 1950.
- Manufacturing (TVs, appliances) has become 90%+ cheaper in labor-time.
- Housing and education---services with limited productivity gains---have become the great life-cost burdens.

**The fundamental inequality:** Global trade creates near-uniform prices for manufactured goods, but wages vary by 60:1. This means identical physical objects cost radically different portions of human lives depending on where that human was born.

## Appendix: Data Sources and Methodology Notes

### Wage Data
- USA: Bureau of Labor Statistics, Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics
- Germany: Federal Statistical Office (Destatis), Eurostat conversion
- UK: Office for National Statistics
- Japan: Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare
- South Korea: Korean Statistical Information Service
- Developing nations: ILO Global Wage Report, World Bank

### Price Data
- Consumer goods: Official statistics, Numbeo, Expatistan
- Housing: National statistics, Zillow (USA), local equivalents
- Healthcare: CMS (USA), WHO, OECD Health Statistics
- Education: College Board (USA), national education ministries
- Historical: BLS historical series, Census Bureau

### Methodology
1. All prices converted to USD at 2024 exchange rates
2. Median (not mean) wages used to avoid executive compensation skew
3. PPP adjustments applied to non-tradable goods (rent, local services)
4. Historical figures use nominal prices and wages (not inflation-adjusted)
5. Labor-hours = Price / Hourly Wage

### Limitations
- Wage data represents formal sector employment; informal sector wages are often lower
- Price variations within countries (urban vs. rural, regional differences) not fully captured
- Quality improvements (e.g., cars, TVs) make pure labor-hour comparisons incomplete
- Exchange rate fluctuations affect cross-country comparisons
