# F-09: Energy — The Currency of Physics

You use the word "energy" all the time. "I don't have the energy for this." "That took a lot of energy." But what IS energy, really? By the end of this lesson, you'll have a clear, physical understanding of what energy is and why it's the single most important concept in all of physics.

## What Is Energy?

Here's the simplest definition: **Energy is the ability to make things happen.**

If you have energy, you can:
- Lift something up
- Push something forward
- Heat something up
- Make a sound
- Turn on a light

If you DON'T have energy, nothing can change. Everything just sits there.

Energy isn't a thing you can hold in your hand. It's not made of atoms. It's more like money: it's a way of keeping track of what's possible.

Think of energy as the CURRENCY of physics. You can spend it to make things happen, you can save it, you can move it from one place to another, but you can never create it out of nothing and you can never make it disappear completely. It just changes forms.

## Potential Energy: Stored Energy

**Potential energy** is energy that's stored up, ready to be used.

Imagine you're holding a basketball above your head. It's not moving. Nothing is happening. But that ball has energy stored in it, because if you let go, it will fall. The higher you hold it, the more energy it has stored.

We call this **gravitational potential energy** (because gravity is what will pull it down).

```
     O  <-- Ball held high
    /|\    (lots of potential energy)
    / \



  ========= Ground
```

If you hold the ball just above the ground, it doesn't have much potential energy. If you hold it way up high, it has a lot.

Another example: a stretched rubber band. You pull it back, and it's storing energy. Let go, and that energy gets released—the rubber band snaps forward.

## Kinetic Energy: Energy of Motion

**Kinetic energy** is the energy something has because it's moving.

A rolling ball has kinetic energy. A flying airplane has kinetic energy. A falling rock has kinetic energy.

The faster something moves, the more kinetic energy it has. A car going 60 mph has way more kinetic energy than the same car going 5 mph.

The heavier something is, the more kinetic energy it has at the same speed. A truck going 30 mph has more kinetic energy than a bicycle going 30 mph.

```
        O -->  Moving ball
              (kinetic energy)
```

## Energy Converts: The Magic Trick

Here's where it gets interesting: **energy can change from one form to another.**

Drop that basketball you were holding:
- At the top: it has potential energy (because it's high up), but no kinetic energy (because it's not moving yet)
- As it falls: potential energy converts into kinetic energy (it's losing height but gaining speed)
- Just before it hits the ground: it has almost no potential energy left (it's almost at ground level), but LOTS of kinetic energy (it's moving fast)

The total energy stays the same—it just changes form.

```
HIGH UP:
   O   <-- Potential energy = HIGH
          Kinetic energy = 0

HALFWAY:
          Potential energy = MEDIUM
   O   <-- Kinetic energy = MEDIUM
          (falling fast now)

GROUND:
          Potential energy = 0
  -O-->  Kinetic energy = HIGH
          (moving fastest)
```

This is happening ALL THE TIME in nature. Energy is constantly converting from one form to another.

## A Pendulum: Energy Swapping Back and Forth

A pendulum is the perfect example of energy conversion.

Pull a pendulum to one side and let go:

```
     \              |              /
      \             |             /
       o           o           o
     (Start)    (Bottom)     (Other side)
```

At the start (pulled to one side):
- High up = lots of potential energy
- Not moving = no kinetic energy

At the bottom (middle of the swing):
- Low down = less potential energy
- Moving fast = lots of kinetic energy

At the other side (highest point again):
- High up again = lots of potential energy again
- Stopped for a moment = no kinetic energy

Then it swings back, and the cycle repeats. The energy keeps converting: potential → kinetic → potential → kinetic, over and over.

## Energy Is Conserved: The Golden Rule

Here's the most important law in all of physics:

**Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only change forms.**

This is called the **Law of Conservation of Energy**, and it's true everywhere, all the time, no exceptions.

When you burn gasoline in a car:
- Chemical energy (stored in the gasoline) converts to kinetic energy (the car moves) and heat energy (the engine gets hot)
- No energy is lost—it just spreads out into different forms

When you eat food:
- Chemical energy (stored in the food) converts to kinetic energy (you move around), heat (your body stays warm), and energy stored in your muscles

When a battery dies:
- Chemical energy converts to electrical energy, then to light (in a flashlight) or sound (in a speaker)

The total amount of energy in the universe never changes. It just keeps changing forms.

## Springs and Elastic Energy

A spring has another kind of potential energy: **elastic energy**.

When you squish a spring (compress it) or stretch it, you're storing energy in it. Let go, and the spring releases that energy—it bounces back to its original shape.

```
Relaxed spring:
  |||||||||
  (no stored energy)

Compressed spring:
  |||
  (elastic energy stored)

Released:
  |||||||||  BOING!
  (energy released as motion)
```

This will be important later, because one of the most important ideas in physics—the harmonic oscillator—is just a ball attached to a spring. We'll get there in the next lesson.

## Energy in Everyday Life

Now that you know what energy is, you can see it everywhere:

**A ball rolling down a hill**: Potential energy (height) converts to kinetic energy (speed).

**A car braking**: Kinetic energy (motion) converts to heat energy (the brakes get hot).

**Charging your phone**: Electrical energy (from the outlet) converts to chemical energy (stored in the battery).

**A candle burning**: Chemical energy (wax and oxygen) converts to light and heat.

**You walking**: Chemical energy (from your breakfast) converts to kinetic energy (your movement) and heat (you warm up when you exercise).

Energy is constantly flowing and changing, but the total amount never changes.

## Practice Exercises

Try these to test your understanding. Answers are at the bottom.

1. You lift a backpack from the floor onto a table. Did the backpack gain potential energy, kinetic energy, or both?

2. A car is rolling down a hill and speeding up. Is potential energy converting to kinetic energy, or kinetic energy converting to potential energy?

3. You compress a spring with your hand. Where does the energy you used go?

4. A ball is rolling across a flat floor and gradually slows down and stops. Energy is conserved, so where did the ball's kinetic energy go?

5. At which point does a swinging pendulum have the MOST kinetic energy:
   - At the highest point on one side
   - At the bottom (middle of the swing)
   - At the highest point on the other side

## You Just Learned the Language of Physics

Energy is THE central idea in physics. Every single thing that happens—motion, heat, light, sound, chemical reactions, even the weird quantum stuff we'll get to soon—can be understood in terms of energy changing forms.

You now understand:
- What energy is (the ability to make things happen)
- Potential energy (stored, waiting to be used)
- Kinetic energy (energy of motion)
- Energy conversion (changing from one form to another)
- Conservation (energy is never created or destroyed, just transformed)

These ideas will appear again and again as we go deeper. You've got the foundation.

## Answers to Exercises

1. The backpack gained **potential energy**. You lifted it higher, so it now has more gravitational potential energy. (It might have had kinetic energy briefly while you were lifting it, but once it's sitting on the table at rest, it only has potential energy.)

2. Potential energy is converting to kinetic energy. The car is losing height (losing potential energy) and gaining speed (gaining kinetic energy).

3. The energy you used went into the spring as **elastic potential energy**. It's now stored in the compressed spring, ready to be released.

4. The ball's kinetic energy converted mostly into **heat energy** (from friction with the floor and air resistance). The floor and air warmed up very slightly. Energy was conserved—it didn't disappear, it just spread out as heat.

5. At the **bottom** (middle of the swing). That's where the pendulum is moving fastest, so that's where it has the most kinetic energy. At the highest points on either side, it stops for a moment, so kinetic energy is zero there.
