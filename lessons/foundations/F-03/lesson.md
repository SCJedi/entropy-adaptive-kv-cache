# F-03: Multiplication and Fractions

Welcome to lesson three! You've learned to count, add, and subtract. Now we're going to learn a shortcut for adding the same number over and over (multiplication), and how to split things into equal parts (fractions). Both of these show up constantly in daily life.

## Multiplication: Repeated Addition

Imagine you're setting up tables for a party. Each table needs 4 chairs. You have 3 tables. How many chairs do you need total?

You could add: 4 + 4 + 4 = 12

Or you could multiply: 3 times 4 = 12

Multiplication is just a faster way to add the same number multiple times.

```
Table 1:  * * * *     (4 chairs)
Table 2:  * * * *     (4 chairs)
Table 3:  * * * *     (4 chairs)
          ────────
Total:    12 chairs
```

We write this as: 3 × 4 = 12

The × symbol means "times" or "groups of."

Read it: "Three times four equals twelve" or "Three groups of four equals twelve."

## How to Think About Multiplication

Here are different ways to understand 3 × 4:

**As groups**: 3 groups, each containing 4 things
**As addition**: 4 + 4 + 4
**As rows and columns**:
```
* * * *
* * * *
* * * *
```
Count them all: 12

## Multiplication Order

Good news: order doesn't matter!

- 3 × 4 = 12
- 4 × 3 = 12

Think about it: 3 groups of 4 chairs gives you the same total as 4 groups of 3 chairs.

```
3×4:          4×3:
* * * *       * * *
* * * *       * * *
* * * *       * * *
              * * *
```

Both give you 12 things total.

## Some Useful Multiplication Facts

**Multiplying by 1**: Any number times 1 equals itself.
- 5 × 1 = 5
- Why? One group of 5 is just 5.

**Multiplying by 0**: Any number times 0 equals 0.
- 5 × 0 = 0
- Why? Zero groups of 5 means you have nothing.

**Multiplying by 2**: Double the number.
- 2 × 6 = 12
- Think: two groups of 6, or 6 + 6

**Multiplying by 10**: Add a zero to the end.
- 10 × 7 = 70
- Why? Ten groups of 7, which is 7 tens, which is 70.

## What Are Fractions?

Fractions are about splitting something into equal parts.

Imagine you have a pizza. You cut it into 4 equal slices. Each slice is "one out of four parts" of the whole pizza.

We write that as: 1/4

Read it: "one fourth" or "one quarter"

```
    ___
   / | \
  /  |  \
 /___|___\   <- A pizza cut into 4 equal parts
 \   |   /      Each slice is 1/4
  \  |  /
   \_|_/
```

The bottom number (4) tells you how many equal parts the whole thing is split into.
The top number (1) tells you how many of those parts you're talking about.

## Common Fractions

**One half (1/2)**: Split into 2 equal parts, take 1.

```
Pizza cut in half:
  ___
 /   \
|     |  <- Each half is 1/2
 \___/
```

If you cut a sandwich into 2 equal pieces, each piece is 1/2 of the sandwich.

**One third (1/3)**: Split into 3 equal parts, take 1.

```
Split into thirds:
 _____
|_____|  <- Each part is 1/3
|_____|
|_____|
```

If three friends share a chocolate bar equally, each friend gets 1/3 of it.

**One fourth (1/4)**: Split into 4 equal parts, take 1.

```
Split into fourths:
 ___
|_|_|  <- Each square is 1/4
|_|_|
```

If four people split a pie equally, each person gets 1/4 of the pie.

## More Than One Part

What if you take 2 pieces out of 4?

That's 2/4 (read: "two fourths").

```
    ___
   /██\      Dark parts = 2/4 of the pizza
  /██ |  \
 /___|___\
```

Here's the pattern:
- 1/4 = one part
- 2/4 = two parts
- 3/4 = three parts
- 4/4 = all four parts = the whole thing = 1

## Fractions in Real Life

**Cooking**: A recipe calls for 1/2 cup of sugar. You fill a measuring cup halfway.

**Time**: It's half past two (1/2 hour past 2 o'clock). The minute hand is halfway around the clock.

**Money**: A quarter is 1/4 of a dollar. Four quarters make one whole dollar.

**Distance**: You've walked 3/4 of a mile. That means the mile is split into 4 parts, and you've walked 3 of them.

## Fractions Equal to 1

Any fraction where the top and bottom numbers are the same equals 1 (the whole thing):
- 2/2 = 1
- 5/5 = 1
- 100/100 = 1

Why? If something is split into 5 parts and you take all 5 parts, you have the whole thing.
