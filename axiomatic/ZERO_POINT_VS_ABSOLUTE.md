# The Zero Point Problem: QFT Sets a Background, Cell Vacuum Doesn't

## The Insight

QFT (mode vacuum) establishes an arbitrary "zero" and measures everything relative to it.

Cell vacuum doesn't need a zero — it has an absolute value.

This is the deepest difference between the two approaches, and it explains almost everything.

---

## What QFT Actually Does

### Step 1: Define the vacuum as zero

In standard QFT, the first thing you do is:

$$:H: \equiv H - \langle 0|H|0\rangle$$

This is "normal ordering." You SUBTRACT the vacuum energy and define the result as your Hamiltonian.

What does this mean? You're saying: "I don't know what the vacuum energy is (it's infinite), so I'll call it zero and measure everything relative to that."

### Step 2: All predictions are differences from this zero

Every QFT prediction is now a deviation from the vacuum:

- Energy of a particle: E = ℏω (relative to vacuum)
- Scattering amplitude: ⟨out|S|in⟩ (both states built on the same vacuum)
- Casimir force: E(d) - E(∞) (difference of vacuum energies at different separations)

You never ask "what IS the vacuum energy?" You only ask "how does this differ from the vacuum?"

### Step 3: This works — for particle physics

This is brilliant for particle physics. All you care about is:
- How much energy does a particle have?
- What's the probability of this scattering?
- How does this state differ from that state?

These are all relative questions. The absolute zero doesn't matter.

### Step 4: This fails — for gravity

Gravity doesn't care about your arbitrary zero. Einstein's equations:

$$G_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}$$

The right side is T_μν — the actual stress-energy, not "stress-energy relative to some reference."

When you ask gravity "what does the vacuum weigh?", it needs an absolute answer. And QFT says: "I set that to zero by definition. I can't tell you."

Or worse: "Before I set it to zero, it was infinite."

## What the Cell Vacuum Does Differently

### No arbitrary zero

The cell vacuum energy density is:

$$\rho_{cell} = \frac{m^4 c^5}{\hbar^3}$$

This isn't "relative to something." It's an absolute number.

For m = 1.77 meV: ρ ≈ 6 × 10⁻¹⁰ J/m³

You can plug this directly into Einstein's equations. No subtraction, no reference state, no "I defined this to be zero."

### Why is it absolute?

Because the construction is absolute:

1. Space is divided into cells of size λ_C = ℏ/mc
2. Each cell has one quantum harmonic oscillator
3. Each oscillator is in a coherent state with energy ℏω/2 = mc²/2
4. Total energy = (number of cells) × (energy per cell)
5. Energy density = mc²/2 ÷ λ_C³ = m⁴c⁵/(2ℏ³)

There's no reference state. There's no "vacuum we subtract." There's just: this many cells, this much energy per cell, this is the total.

### The coherent state isn't "nothing"

In the mode vacuum, |0⟩ is defined as "no particles" — it's the state with nothing in it. Then you find this "nothing" has infinite energy, so you subtract it.

In the cell vacuum, |Ω⟩ = ⊗|α⟩ is NOT "nothing." It's a specific state with a specific energy. Each cell has half a quantum of excitation. The vacuum is a thing, not an absence.

## The Philosophical Difference

### QFT: The vacuum is a reference point

"I don't know what the vacuum is, and I don't care. I'll call it zero and measure everything else relative to it."

This is operationalism: only differences are observable, so only differences matter.

### Cell vacuum: The vacuum is a thing

"The vacuum is a specific state with a specific energy. I can tell you exactly what it is: ρ = m⁴c⁵/ℏ³."

This is realism: the vacuum has an absolute existence, not just a relational one.

## Why This Matters

### For particle physics: doesn't matter

Particle physics only asks relative questions. Both approaches give the same answers for scattering, decays, energy differences.

### For cosmology: matters enormously

Cosmology asks: "What is the energy density of the universe?"

QFT says: "I set the vacuum to zero. If you want the actual vacuum energy... uh... it's either 0 (by definition) or ∞ (before I subtracted)."

Cell vacuum says: "ρ = m⁴c⁵/ℏ³ ≈ 6 × 10⁻¹⁰ J/m³."

One gives you a number. One doesn't.

### For the CC problem: this IS the problem

The cosmological constant problem is often stated as: "QFT predicts vacuum energy of 10¹²³ GeV⁴, but we observe 10⁻⁴⁷ GeV⁴."

But that's not quite right. QFT doesn't predict 10¹²³ — it predicts infinity, or cutoff-dependent, or "undefined until you subtract something."

The real problem is: **QFT doesn't predict the vacuum energy at all.** It sets up a framework where only differences are meaningful, then we ask it an absolute question.

The cell vacuum solves this by being a framework where absolute values ARE meaningful.

## The Analogy: Voltage vs. Energy

### Voltage (like QFT)

In electromagnetism, only voltage DIFFERENCES matter. You can set the "zero" of electric potential anywhere you want. The physics is the same.

If someone asks "what is the absolute electric potential of this point?", the answer is: "That's not a meaningful question. Only differences are physical."

### Energy in gravity (like cell vacuum needs)

But gravity is different. The absolute energy density determines spacetime curvature. You can't just "set it to zero" — that changes the physics.

If someone asks "what is the absolute energy density here?", gravity needs an answer. And QFT says: "I only do differences."

## The Mathematical Structure

### QFT: Quotient space

Mathematically, QFT works in a quotient space. You take the space of all states and identify states that differ only by a constant (the vacuum energy).

Observable space = (raw space) / (constant shifts)

This is why only differences are defined.

### Cell vacuum: Actual space

The cell vacuum works in the actual space. The state |Ω⟩ has a specific energy, not "energy modulo a constant."

There's no quotient. Values are absolute.

---

## What This Explains

### Why renormalization works

Renormalization works because particle physics only needs the quotient space. Subtracting infinity is fine if you only care about differences.

### Why the CC problem exists

The CC problem exists because gravity needs the actual space, not the quotient. When you ask an absolute question of a quotient-space theory, you get nonsense (0 or ∞).

### Why cell vacuum gives finite answers

The cell vacuum gives finite answers because it's defined in the actual space, not the quotient. The energy density is a number, not an equivalence class.

### Why cell vacuum has w = 0

The mode vacuum, by setting itself to "zero," implicitly claims ρ = 0 and p = 0, giving w = 0/0 (undefined) or w = -1 (if you interpret the cosmological constant as vacuum energy).

The cell vacuum has ρ = m⁴c⁵/ℏ³ and p = 0 (from the virial theorem), giving w = 0.

The cell vacuum isn't "zero energy pretending to be something." It's actual energy that happens to have zero pressure.

## The Deep Point

### QFT asks: "How does this differ from the vacuum?"

It's a theory of excitations above a background. The background itself is undefined (or defined to be zero by fiat).

### Cell vacuum asks: "What is there?"

It's a theory of what exists. The vacuum is a specific state with specific properties. There's no background you subtract — there's just reality.
