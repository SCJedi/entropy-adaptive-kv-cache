# Primer: What Are Axioms and Why Physics Needs Them

## The Big Idea

An axiom is a foundational rule you accept without proof. It's the starting point -- the bedrock on which you build everything else. In mathematics and physics, axioms define what counts as a valid statement or theory before you start calculating.

The most famous example is Euclid's geometry. Around 300 BCE, Euclid wrote down five axioms for flat geometry. For two thousand years, mathematicians tried to prove the fifth axiom (the parallel postulate) from the first four. They failed. In the 1800s, mathematicians realized you could replace it with a different axiom and get a different but equally consistent geometry -- curved space instead of flat.

The lesson: axioms are choices, not eternal truths. Change the axioms, and you change the entire theory.

## Why Physics Needs Axioms

Physics uses axioms to define what counts as a valid theory. Newton's three laws of motion are axioms of classical mechanics. The Schrödinger equation (or the Hilbert space postulates) is an axiom of quantum mechanics. Einstein's equivalence principle and field equations are axioms of general relativity.

These axioms aren't derived from something more fundamental. They're the foundation. You test them by deriving consequences and comparing to experiment. If the predictions match reality, the axioms are useful. If not, you revise them.

Quantum field theory (QFT) has axioms too -- the Wightman axioms define what makes a theory relativistic, local, and causal. But these axioms don't solve the cosmological constant problem. They don't tell you which vacuum state the universe is in, or what the vacuum energy density is.

We need additional axioms for that.

## The Problem in Quantum Field Theory

In QFT, every field mode is a quantum harmonic oscillator. Each oscillator has zero-point energy $\hbar\omega/2$. Add up all the modes, and you get:

$$E_0 = \sum_{\text{all modes}} \frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega \to \infty$$

Infinite total energy. Even with a cutoff at the Planck scale, you get a vacuum energy density $10^{123}$ times larger than what astronomers observe. This is the "cosmological constant problem."

Standard QFT has no axiom that says "the vacuum energy must be finite" or "the vacuum must be the state of minimum energy density consistent with symmetries." It just doesn't address the question.

## What This Course Does

This course proposes seven axioms for vacuum selection. The first four are standard (relativistic covariance, locality, positive energy, Hilbert space structure). The last three are new:

5. **Discrete Translational Symmetry** -- The vacuum respects a fundamental length scale.
6. **Entropy Minimization** -- The vacuum is the purest possible state.
7. **Finite Energy Density** -- The vacuum energy per unit volume is a finite number.

These axioms are designed to uniquely select one vacuum state out of infinitely many possibilities. If they succeed, they solve the cosmological constant problem by construction. If they fail (by being inconsistent, or by contradicting experiment), they need revision.

Either way, we get clarity.

## Consistency vs. Truth

A critical distinction: axioms can be internally consistent without being true.

Euclidean geometry is logically flawless. Every theorem follows from the axioms. But physical space is curved, not flat. Euclidean geometry is a beautiful, rigorous, self-consistent system that happens to be wrong about the universe.

The same applies here. The seven axioms might be perfectly consistent with each other and still fail to describe reality. The only way to find out is to derive their consequences and test them against observation.
