# F-01: What Is a Rule?

Welcome! You're about to learn something you use every single day without even thinking about it. By the end of this lesson, you'll understand what rules really are and why they're so important. You can do this.

## Rules You Already Know

You follow rules all the time. When you play a game, read a recipe, or even just walk down the street, you're using rules.

Let's look at some examples:

**Playing Tag:**
- One person is "it"
- If "it" touches you, you become "it"
- You can't tag someone who just tagged you (no tag-backs)

**Chess:**
- The knight moves in an L-shape
- The king moves one square at a time
- You can't move into check

**Traffic Lights:**
- Red means stop
- Green means go
- Yellow means slow down (get ready to stop)

These are all rules. But what makes something a rule?

## What Makes Something a Rule?

A rule is an instruction that tells you how things work or what to do in a situation.

Good rules have three important features:

```
CLEAR: You can understand what the rule says
CONSISTENT: The rule works the same way every time
APPLIES TO SITUATIONS: The rule tells you what to do when
```

Let's check the tag rule "If 'it' touches you, you become 'it'":

- Clear? Yes. We know what "touch" and "it" mean.
- Consistent? Yes. This happens every time, not just sometimes.
- Applies to situations? Yes. It tells us what happens WHEN someone gets touched.

## Two Kinds of Rules

Some rules describe how things ACTUALLY work. Other rules describe how things SHOULD work.

**Rules You Can't Break (Natural Rules):**

These are rules about how the world works. You can't choose to ignore them.

- Dropped things fall down (gravity)
- Fire is hot
- 2 + 2 = 4
- Ice melts when it gets warm

No matter how hard you try, you can't make dropped things fall UP. The universe just works this way.

**Rules You Could Break (But Shouldn't):**

These are rules people made to help things work well. You COULD break them, but there are consequences.

- Don't cheat in games
- Raise your hand before speaking in class
- Don't run with scissors
- Wait your turn in line

You CAN run with scissors (physically possible), but it's dangerous, so we have a rule against it.

In math and science, we mostly care about the first kind: rules that describe how things actually work.

## Why Rules Help

Imagine playing a game where the rules changed every minute:

```
Minute 1: Knights move in an L-shape
Minute 2: Knights can now fly anywhere on the board
Minute 3: Knights don't exist anymore
Minute 4: Knights are back, but they can only move backwards
```

This would be chaos! You couldn't plan. You couldn't learn. You couldn't predict what would happen next.

Rules help because:

**Everyone knows what to expect**: If 2 + 2 always equals 4, you can use that fact to build bigger ideas.

**You can make plans**: If you know the rules, you can figure out what will happen next.

**You can check your work**: If you get 2 + 2 = 5, you know you made a mistake because the rule says it should be 4.

**You can communicate**: When we all agree on the same rules, we can talk to each other and be understood.

## Rules in Math

Math is built on rules. Here are some examples:

**Addition always works the same way:**
- 3 + 5 = 8 (today, tomorrow, forever)
- 5 + 3 = 8 (the order doesn't matter)

**Multiplication is repeated addition:**
- 4 × 3 means "add 4 to itself 3 times"
- 4 + 4 + 4 = 12
- So 4 × 3 = 12

**Even numbers end in 0, 2, 4, 6, or 8:**
- 10, 22, 34, 46, 58 are all even
- 11, 23, 35, 47, 59 are all odd

These rules never change. That's what makes math so powerful. You can trust it.

## Finding the Rules

Sometimes the rules aren't written down. You have to figure them out by watching what happens.

Look at this pattern:

```
2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, ...
```

What's the rule? Each number is 2 more than the one before it. We're counting by twos.

What comes next? Following the rule: 14, 16, 18...

Try this one:

```
1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, ...
```

This is trickier. The rule is: add the previous two numbers to get the next one.
- 1 + 1 = 2
- 1 + 2 = 3
- 2 + 3 = 5
- 3 + 5 = 8
- 5 + 8 = 13

What comes next? 8 + 13 = 21. (This is called the Fibonacci sequence, and it shows up in nature all the time!)
